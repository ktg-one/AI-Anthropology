Building an audience is hard.

And after doing this myself — and helping hundreds of creators do the same — I’ve recognized a few patterns.

This is the checklist I wish someone had given me when I started. If you follow it, you’ll move faster than 99% of people trying to build an audience.

## 1\. Start with this (or struggle forever)

You don’t need a lot of time.

But you absolutely need the right foundation. And that foundation is knowing who you’re writing for.

Ask yourself:

- What type of audience do I want to serve?
- Who is the best kind of person I’d like to help?
- Who do I enjoy dealing with, and who has the money to pay me?

It all starts here.

The problem is that many of you are stubborn and want to write about everything that interests you. Or serve 321 different personas at once.

I’m not saying it’s easy.

It’s hard. Because you’ll voluntarily give 99% of your potential readers the finger.

A few years back, I was working with an eye surgeon who had been helping diabetics for over three decades. But as we kept niching down, we realized he could only serve a specific type of diabetic. And that specific diabetic became his niche.

I focus on part-time creators.

People with knowledge and expertise who have a 9–5 or run a traditional offline business and want to take it online. They’re at least 35 years old. They’re not kids trying to get rich by next Tuesday.

This eliminated:

- Full-time creators already making six figures
- 18-year-olds who want to drive a Lambo and let ChatGPT write their content half-drunk

Riches are in the niches.

## 2\. What’s the best way to pay you?

The biggest mistake you can make is creating a business you’ll hate.

I started making real money online through freelancing and delivering services. This taught me something: I don’t want to work for clients.

Then I dipped my toes into e-commerce, and that taught me something else: I don’t want to manage inventory, suppliers, and customer service.

But I’ve always had a knack for learning. I love creating and buying courses. That’s why I consciously designed my business around this model.

So ask yourself:

- Do I want to offer a service?
- Do I prefer creating digital products?
- Am I more of a cohort or workshop person?

You might not know all the parameters upfront. That’s fine.

Start with a north star — what makes the most sense to you right now. As you build, you might realize you don’t resonate with a particular audience or approach.

If that happens, adjust.

## 3\. The one thing most people never understand

Not everything is created equal.

Most people fall into one of two traps:

- They spend too much time building products no one buys because they don’t have an audience.
- Or they spend too much time building an audience without having anything to sell.

The goal is to find one thing: balance.

When you’re starting out, don’t waste time creating products. Start with coaching. [You can whip up a coaching offer in 15 minutes. The formula is in this free cheat sheet.](https://tally.so/r/m61lOO?id=2026-01-03&interest=dp)

**In the first 1–2 years, focus most of your time on building an audience.**

Even if your first product flops, you’ll still have an audience to sell something else to.

## 4\. Master the boring basics

To build an audience, you need to post on platforms.

Think of each platform as a playing field. Every playing field has its own rules. And it blows my mind how many people step onto the field without knowing them.

For example:

- **YouTube** requires thumbnails, titles, and a strong first 30 seconds.
- **Substack** requires short-form hooks, long-form titles, and solid intros.
- **SEO** requires keyword research and optimized writing.

But underneath all of this are two fundamental skills:

**1\. Packaging** — The angle you choose for your content. One idea can be approached from many angles.

**2\. Structuring** — Once you have your angle, structure your content properly. This makes everything else easier.

## 5\. Take publishing seriously

Most people plan to publish. But they never do it consistently.

When you’re just getting started, you need to post something five days a week.

Minimum.

Writing and publishing content will teach you more than anything else.

The more you post, the more feedback you’ll get. And the faster you’ll improve.

You might think this takes too much time. It doesn’t.

All you need is a 500-word article. The average person speaks at about 150 words per minute. Can you talk for five minutes straight?

Add some editing. Some copy-pasting. Call it 30–60 minutes total.

Don’t have 30 to 60 minutes a day to build your business?

Stop reading. Go buy a lottery ticket instead.

## Ego is the enemy

Most people struggle to grow because of an overinflated ego. They insist on doing things their way, even when it clearly isn’t working.

Sometimes, you need to stop overthinking and follow a proven playbook until you get results.

Put your head down. Listen to what has worked for others. Apply it.

You don’t need to reinvent the wheel. You need to build momentum within your own stratosphere.

Once you have traction — once you’ve published 100 pieces of content, built your first 1,000 subscribers, and made your first sales — then you can experiment. Then you can innovate.

But not before.

If you’re willing to do that, I’m rooting for you.
